01 Overview

The Challenge

Bike Works is a South Seattle nonprofit that uses bicycling as a vehicle for youth empowerment and social justice — running free youth drop-ins, an Earn-a-Bike program, and a community bike shop on a sliding scale. Their mission is powerful; their digital presence wasn't keeping up.

A project discovery audit and three user interviews revealed an outdated site with accessibility failures, a confusing dual-nav, information hierarchy problems, and brand inconsistency that made it hard for first-time visitors to understand what Bike Works even does.

02 Problem & Solution
The Problem
How might we rebuild Bike Works' visual language to be accessible, clear, and community-authentic — without losing the bold identity that makes them recognizable?

The existing yellow-background site failed accessibility contrast tests, confused new visitors with unclear information hierarchy, relied on two overlapping nav bars, and had no consistent component system guiding layout decisions across pages.

The Solution
A comprehensive, research-justified design system with every token, component, and pattern traced back to a specific user or research finding.

Rather than redesigning by intuition, every decision — from demoting yellow to an accent-only color, to the single consolidated nav, to the 4px spacing grid — is documented with the precise interview quote, audit finding, or academic source that motivated it.

03 Research Foundation

Four research inputs, one system

🗣️
User Interviews (×3)
Michael Lee (bike commuter), Leo Fan (university student), and Roderick McClain (Comms & Grants Manager). Each surface distinct needs: clarity for new visitors, hierarchy for scanners, and accessibility for internal stakeholders.
🏗️
Project Discovery Audit
Identified 7 structural UX failures: yellow accessibility failures, dual nav confusion, information overload, no consistent content structure, uneven spacing, buried identity, and excessively long text-heavy pages.
🔍
Competitor Analysis
Analyzed Banana Boat, Sunny D, Gregg's Cycle, Montlake Bicycle Shop, and Mello Fellos for color system decisions, component patterns, and design system coherence. Each comparison directly informed a system decision.
📚
Academic Literature (×14)
Papers on cycling equity, bike advocacy in underserved communities, sustainable transportation, and community trust-building. Confirmed that visible impact data and community-authentic messaging are critical for Bike Works' audience.
♿
WCAG Accessibility Review
Every color pairing in the system was evaluated against WCAG AA contrast ratios. Yellow (#F5C518) was demoted to accent-only after failing as a large background color — a change directly requested by internal stakeholders.
🚲
Bike Works Mission Alignment
Values: Bicycling, Access, Youth, Environment, Community, Social Justice, Education. Every component — from the FREE badge prominence to the sliding scale alert — serves the mission of equitable access for underserved South Seattle communities.
04 Key Research Findings
01

New visitors can't identify what Bike Works does. Michael Lee said "as an organization, it's hard to understand what they are — volunteers, taking donations, a little bit weird." The hero pattern was redesigned to answer who/what/for whom within the first viewport.

02

Yellow-as-background fails accessibility and creates reading fatigue. Roderick McClain flagged that "bold yellow background will likely fail accessibility tests." Leo Fan noted that "color contrast works, but extended reading is tiring." Yellow was demoted to accent-only use.

03

Free and low-cost services are the most compelling content. Leo Fan was "immediately drawn to Free and Low-Cost Services" and found programs like Earn-a-Bike "highly valuable." The FREE badge became the most visually prominent badge in the entire system.

04

Two nav bars confuse user journeys. The site audit directly identified "the website contains two nav bars, which could confuse the user and their journey." The system consolidates everything — links, icons, search, Support CTA — into one bar.

05

Uneven spacing signals an unpolished, untrustworthy product. The audit noted "a lot of uneven spacing in the left side" and footer link spacing issues. A strict 4px base spacing scale eliminates all ad-hoc values, creating visual rhythm that builds credibility.

06

Community-authentic organizations must communicate impact to build trust. Academic research on cycling equity found that youth and low-income communities disengage when organizations don't clearly demonstrate tangible impact. Stat cards (300+ bikes, FREE, 27 years) directly address this.

06 Key Design Decisions

Research to decision

Component Decision Made Research Source

Competitor: Mello Fellos — "yellow used sparingly as accent only."
Navigation Single consolidated nav bar replacing dual-nav system; Support CTA always visible Project discovery audit: "The website contains two nav bars, which could confuse the user and their journey."
Roderick McClain: donation promotion needs visibility.
Typography Barlow Condensed for display; 16px body with 1.7 line-height; strict 7-step type scale Leo Fan: "felt the site contained a lot of text — suggested communicating key information faster."
WCAG 1.4.8: line spacing minimum 1.5× — exceeded.

07 System Deliverables

What the system includes

🎨
Foundation Tokens
The complete visual DNA — every value needed to implement the system in code.
  • 10-color primary palette with WCAG compliance labels
  • 6-step neutral gray scale
  • 3 semantic colors (success, warning, error)
  • 9-step spacing scale on 4px base grid
  • 5 border radius values from 4px to pill
  • CSS custom properties for all tokens
🔠
Typography System
7-level type scale with usage guidelines and rationale for each step.
  • Display / H1 / H2 / H3 / Eyebrow / Body / Caption
  • Barlow Condensed for display contexts
  • Barlow for all body reading
  • Accessible line-height and size minimums
🧩
UI Components
Production-ready components with all interactive states documented.
  • 5 button variants × 4 sizes × 3 states
  • Form inputs, selects, textarea, checkboxes
  • Program, stat, horizontal, and alert cards
  • Navigation bar, breadcrumb, tabs, pagination
  • Badges, status indicators, filter tags
  • Icon library with 16 community-relevant icons
📐
Assembled Patterns
4 full-section patterns showing components in real page context.
  • Hero section — new visitor orientation
  • Earn-a-Bike progress tracker
  • Donation CTA — sliding scale communication
  • Schedule listing — where to find us
  • Each annotated with its specific research justification
08 Reflection & Takeaways
Key Takeaway
"A design system isn't just a component library — it's an argument for every decision, made visible."
Research-first design produces defensible work
Documenting the specific user quote or audit finding behind every token forced me to question vague intuitions. If I couldn't cite a source for a decision, that was a signal to either do more research or reconsider the decision entirely.
Accessibility and brand identity aren't in conflict
The yellow accessibility failure could have felt like a loss of brand identity. Instead, restricting yellow to accent-only made it more powerful — more visually impactful as a CTA color than it ever was as a background. Constraints produce better design.
Design systems for nonprofits must earn community trust
Reading the academic literature on cycling equity and bike advocacy in underserved communities changed how I thought about hierarchy decisions. Surfacing "FREE" and "300+ bikes" as primary signals wasn't just a UX choice — it was about making the organization's equity mission legible to the communities it serves.
Portfolio annotation transforms work into process evidence
Building the rationale panels directly into the design system document means anyone reviewing this work can see not just what was built, but why — demonstrating the full research-to-decision-to-component loop that separates UX thinking from visual styling.
