WayPoint:
Travel Diary

A mobile travel diary app that transforms scattered photos, notes, and plans into one cohesive travel narrative — helping people capture memories and find inspiration for their next adventure.

01 Overview

The Challenge

Travel memories and plans are messy. People use different apps for photos, notes, and planning — making it hard to find and appreciate past trips or prepare efficiently for new ones.

Existing apps solve parts of the problem (navigation, recommendations, photo storage) but none provide a single emotional home for the full travel experience — from planning through reflection.

4+ Apps the average traveler uses to plan and document a trip
3 Traveler personas researched: Planner, Memory Keeper, Social Sharer
1 Pivotal scope change driven by peer feedback mid-project
4 Core screens designed: Feed, Diary, Journal, Future Travels
02 Problem & Solution
The Problem
How might we make it simple for travelers to organize their photos, notes, and future plans in one central location?

Travelers rely on a fragmented ecosystem — Google Maps, Pinterest, Notes apps, browser bookmarks — to plan and remember trips. Important links get lost, memories feel scattered, and there's no space for personal reflection alongside the visuals.

The Solution
A single app where you can write your travel diary, store your photos, and make future plans.

WayPoint puts everything in one place — a community-focused journaling app where travelers can post photo dumps, write journal entries, and save future destinations. Public/private toggles let users balance personal reflection with community inspiration.

03 UX Research

Understanding how people travel and remember

🎙
User Interviews
Conducted interviews with frequent travelers, casual vacationers, and study abroad students to understand documentation behaviors, planning habits, and what motivates people to record and share their experiences.
🔍
Competitive Analysis
Reviewed Polarsteps, TripIt, and Pinterest to identify strengths, gaps, and unmet opportunities. Key finding: no existing app combines emotional journaling with visual documentation and future planning in a single experience.
🗺
Journey Mapping
Mapped the end-to-end travel experience — planning, traveling, and reflecting — to identify support opportunities at each stage. Revealed that the "reflection" phase was entirely unserved by existing tools.
👤
Persona Development
Created three traveler personas to guide design decisions: The Planner (detail-oriented, itinerary-driven), The Memory Keeper (photo-first, reflective), and The Social Sharer (spontaneous, community-motivated).

04 Key Insights
01

Travelers document differently. Some prefer visual logs (photos, highlights), while others prefer long-form reflective writing — validating the need for both a Diary and a Journal feature in the same app.

02

Planning tools are scattered. Users rely on Google Maps, Notes, Pinterest, and browser bookmarks simultaneously — causing loss of inspiration and important links when it's time to actually travel.

03

Sharing is selective. People want to share travel highlights publicly but keep personal reflections private — validating the need for flexible, per-entry visibility controls.

04

Low-friction entry is critical. Users want to quickly jot down notes or upload photos without navigating complex menus — any friction at capture time means memories are lost.

05

Memory preservation matters. Users deeply value being able to visually revisit past trips — this informed the image-first design of diary entries and the "Future Travels" destination tracker.

05 Design Process & Pivot

A scope change that made the app better

The original proposal was a multi-functional travel app helping users find local activities and navigate public transportation, with a travel diary as a secondary feature. Peer feedback revealed this was too ambitious — the utility features were superficial replications of existing apps, and the core purpose was getting lost.

The Pivot
"I decided to lean into what travel means to me: creating memories and having an outlet to express our thoughts and feelings."
Original Proposal
Multi-functional travel utility

Activity finder, public transit navigation, and a travel diary as an add-on. Too broad, with superficial utility features that already existed in better-executed apps — causing a confusing user experience.

Final Direction
Memory-keeping & community journaling

A focused "minimum lovable product" centered on capturing and sharing travel memories. Photo dumps, journal entries, and public/private toggles — every feature directly serves one purpose: preserving and sharing the experience of travel.

06 Design Outcomes

Four screens, one travel compass

🏠
Homepage Feed
Explore new destinations and stay connected with the travel community — a social newsfeed alongside curated destination recommendations.
  • Browse featured destinations with category filters (Adventure, Individual, Group, Family)
  • Social newsfeed with photos, captions, likes, comments, and sharing
  • Curated travel highlights relevant to the user's interests
  • Bottom nav for quick access to all core screens
📔
Travel Diary
Organize and relive past trips through visual, categorized entries — the photo album at the heart of WayPoint.
  • Trips organized across Public, Private, and Draft categories
  • Each entry shows photos, dates, captions, and trip summaries
  • Share, edit, or move posts between visibility categories
  • Image-first layout designed for visual memory revisiting
✍️
Journal
Long-form reflections and storytelling — a space for the feelings and impressions that photos alone can't capture.
  • Narrative entries with timestamps, headings, and organized paragraphs
  • Filter public or private entries; search by tags
  • Tags like #FirstImpression, #Tip, #NewPlace for quick categorization
  • Ongoing personal travel log that complements the visual Diary
🌍
Future Travels
Save and track upcoming destinations — replacing the scattered Pinterest boards, bookmarks, and Notes app links travelers currently use.
  • Destinations categorized by status: Not Visited, In Progress, Visited
  • Add external links, notes, and references for trip research
  • Photos and descriptions for each saved location
  • Update visit status as plans evolve
Browse featured destinations and a social newsfeed with posts from other travelers
Document past trips with photos, dates, captions, and shareable entries
Write short or long-form reflections with tags and public/private visibility
Save and organize upcoming destinations with notes, links, and visit status
Low-friction entry: quick add from any screen via bottom navigation
Flexible visibility controls: public sharing with private personal reflections
07 Reflection & Takeaways
Key Takeaway
"Define the minimum lovable product first, then build the plan around that core concept — not the other way around."
Embrace the pivot — constraints create clarity
De-scoping the project mid-process was uncomfortable but transformative. Removing the navigation and activity features forced a deeper focus on what made WayPoint truly unique: the intersection of personal reflection and community inspiration in travel.
Peer feedback is the most valuable design tool
The critique that the original app was "too much and confusing" was the single most impactful input of the project. It led directly to a more coherent, meaningful product — reinforcing that honest feedback early beats polished work built on a flawed premise.
Coherence comes from a single, clear purpose
Every feature in the final design — Photo Dumps, Journal Entries, the Public/Private Toggle, Future Travels — directly serves the central goal of capturing and sharing travel memories. That focus is what makes the product feel intentional rather than assembled.
