01 Project Overview

The Problem

ICHS's current website suffers from cluttered design and an overwhelming user experience. Patients — especially those with limited English proficiency or low digital literacy — struggle to find services, leading to high call volumes for information that's already available online.

This places real burden on front desk representatives, providers, and call center staff who field questions that a well-designed Services page could answer on its own. Our Capstone team is designing the solution that ICHS will implement in their 2027 website revamp.

02 Problem & Solution
The Problem
A cluttered experience that fails the patients who need it most.

ICHS serves a multilingual, multicultural community — yet its website's information architecture makes it difficult for non-English speakers and low digital literacy users to navigate basic services. The result: patients call in for help instead, creating pressure on staff and barriers to care.

The Solution
A redesigned Services page built around the people ICHS actually serves.

We're delivering high-fidelity Figma prototypes, functional coded implementations, and detailed technical handoff documentation — all built on user research with ICHS patients and Community Access Specialists. The redesign prioritizes intuitive navigation, accessibility, and multilingual support from the ground up.

05 Design Process

From research to prototype

01

Stakeholder research with ICHS staff. We conducted interviews with Community Access Specialists who interact with patients daily — understanding exactly what questions patients ask by phone that the website should be answering, and what barriers they observe in real-time.

02

Audit of the existing Services page. We identified critical usability failures: overwhelming information density, unclear service categorization, no location-based filtering, and navigation patterns that assume digital fluency ICHS's patient population doesn't always have.

03

Information architecture redesign. We restructured the Services page hierarchy to reduce cognitive load — grouping services in ways that match how patients think about their health needs, not how ICHS organizes departments internally.

04

High-fidelity prototyping in Figma. Iterating from low-fi wireframes through mid-fi critiques to high-fidelity mockups, with each round informed by team feedback and alignment with ICHS brand guidelines and accessibility requirements.

05

Functional code implementation. We built a working coded version of the redesigned Services page in HTML, CSS, JavaScript, and React — giving ICHS's development team a concrete implementation reference alongside the Figma files, not just visual mockups.

09 Reflection & Learnings So Far
Capstone Insight
"Designing for accessibility isn't a checklist — it's a commitment to the people who are most often left out."
Real-world constraints make better designers
Working with an actual client — ICHS — means navigating brand guidelines, stakeholder priorities, and implementation timelines alongside user needs. It's sharpened my ability to advocate for the user while building trust and alignment with decision-makers.
Accessibility is a design foundation, not a feature
Targeting WCAG 2.1 AA from the start has changed how I approach every design decision — color contrast, touch target sizes, reading order, language clarity. It's made the work better for everyone, not just users with disabilities.
Collaboration across disciplines requires shared language
Working across design and frontend development within the same team has reinforced how important it is to document decisions clearly and build with implementation in mind. The gap between a beautiful Figma file and working, accessible code is narrowed by communication.
